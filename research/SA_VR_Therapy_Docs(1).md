## PART 1: RESEARCH FOUNDATION

### 1. Understanding Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD)

#### 1.1 Definition and Diagnostic Criteria

Social Anxiety Disorder is a persistent, pathological fear of social or performance situations where a person is exposed to possible scrutiny by others. It goes far beyond normal shyness and is characterized by:

- **Marked and persistent fear** of being negatively evaluated or embarrassed
- **Fear of judgment** in social situations, public performances, or when unfamiliar people are present
- **Anticipated social scrutiny** causing significant anxiety
- Recognition that the fear is excessive or unreasonable (in adults)
- Active avoidance or endurance with intense anxiety/distress
- Duration of 6+ months causing clinically significant distress
- Impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning

**Key distinction from shyness:** SAD is not simply being shy. It involves intense physiological arousal, avoidance behaviors, and significant functional impairment in daily life.

#### 1.2 Prevalence and Statistics

- **Global prevalence:** 7-13% of the general population experiences SAD at some point
- **Adolescents/Young adults in China:** 2.1% with SAD; 23.5% with social anxiety symptoms
- **Most common onset:** Late adolescence to early adulthood (15-25 years old)
- **Gender distribution:** Slightly more common in females
- **Social impact:** Impairs occupational functioning, educational achievement, and interpersonal relationships
- **Comorbidity risk:** High rates of depression, dysthymia, substance abuse, and other anxiety disorders

#### 1.3 Symptoms and Manifestations

**Cognitive Symptoms:**
- Fear of negative evaluation (FNE) from others
- Fear of positive evaluation (FPE) - paradoxically, positive feedback can trigger anxiety
- Negative self-appraisal and self-monitoring
- Catastrophic thinking patterns
- Overestimation of social threat probability
- Rumination about past social interactions
- Anticipatory anxiety about future social situations

**Physical/Physiological Symptoms:**
- Blushing and facial flushing
- Excessive sweating
- Heart palpitations and rapid heartbeat
- Trembling or shaking
- Muscle tension
- Shortness of breath/dyspnea
- Nausea or stomach upset
- Dizziness

**Behavioral Symptoms:**
- Avoidance of social situations
- Avoidance of eye contact
- Difficulty speaking in public
- Hesitation to speak up in groups
- Avoidance of eating/drinking in public
- Safety behaviors (holding objects, standing near exits)
- Self-medication with alcohol or drugs

#### 1.4 Neurobiology of Social Anxiety

**Brain Regions Implicated:**

1. **Amygdala (Extended Amygdala):** 
   - Hyperactivation in response to social threat
   - Enhanced fear processing
   - Activity increases with symptom severity
   - Central to threat detection and fear conditioning

2. **Prefrontal Cortex (PFC):**
   - Reduced activity/connectivity with amygdala
   - Responsible for emotion regulation and threat appraisal
   - Impaired ability to regulate fear responses

3. **Hippocampus:**
   - Involved in contextual fear learning
   - Memory consolidation of social threats
   - Integration with amygdala in fear conditioning

4. **Striatum (Nucleus Accumbens):**
   - Reward processing region
   - Dopamine dysfunction
   - Reduced reward sensitivity to social interactions
   - Associated with submissive/avoidant behaviors

5. **Paralimbic and Limbic Circuits:**
   - Overall increased connectivity indicating heightened emotional responsiveness
   - Automatic threat processing of disorder-related stimuli

**Neurotransmitter Systems:**

1. **Serotonin (5-HT):**
   - Dysregulation implicated in SAD pathogenesis
   - Increased activity in fear-related brain regions
   - Target of SSRI medications

2. **Dopamine:**
   - Lower striatal dopamine reuptake in SAD patients
   - Linked to reward pathway dysfunction
   - Contributes to avoidance and submissive behavior
   - Associated with anhedonia (reduced pleasure in social situations)

3. **Glutamate:**
   - Excitatory neurotransmitter dysfunction
   - Implicated in fear conditioning and extinction learning

4. **GABA:**
   - Inhibitory neurotransmitter
   - Reduced GABAergic inhibition contributes to anxiety
   - Target of anti-anxiety medications

**Genetic and Environmental Factors:**

- **Genetic component:** 40-50% heritability
- **Serotonin transporter polymorphism (SLC6A4):** 
  - Short (S) allele carriers show heightened anxiety responses
  - Interact with environmental stress
  
- **Early life experiences:**
  - Overcontrolling or intrusive parenting
  - Parental anxiety modeling
  - Social rejection or bullying
  - Maternal stress/separation

**Automatic Threat Processing:**

- SAD involves automatic, preconscious processing of social threat
- Threat stimuli are detected rapidly before conscious awareness
- Gaze avoidance: Difficulty maintaining eye contact
- Hypervigilance to social cues
- Negative interpretation bias of ambiguous social signals

#### 1.5 The Avoidance Cycle and Maintenance

The maintenance of social anxiety follows a vicious cycle:

```
Social Situation Trigger
        ↓
Cognitive Appraisal (Overestimate threat, underestimate coping)
        ↓
Anxiety/Fear Response (Physical + Emotional)
        ↓
Safety Behaviors (Eye contact avoidance, muscle tension, silence)
        ↓
Avoidance/Escape
        ↓
Negative Reinforcement (Anxiety temporarily decreases)
        ↓
Increased Fear of Similar Situations
        ↓
[CYCLE REPEATS - Anxiety strengthens]
```

**Why it perpetuates:**
- Avoidance prevents disconfirmation of feared outcomes
- Safety behaviors maintain anxiety (prevent habituation)
- Avoidance strengthens the brain's fear association
- No opportunity to learn that feared outcomes don't occur
- Reduced social skill development from less practice

### 2. Understanding Exposure Therapy for Anxiety

#### 2.1 Theoretical Foundation

Exposure therapy is grounded in **Pavlovian extinction learning** - the process of repeatedly encountering a stimulus without the feared outcome, leading to extinction of the conditioned fear response.

**Core Learning Mechanisms:**

1. **Habituation:**
   - Automatic decrease in physiological arousal with repeated exposure
   - Body becomes accustomed to anxiety trigger
   - Anxiety response naturally decreases over time in presence of feared stimulus
   - *Timeline:* 20-30 minutes typically needed for significant reduction

2. **Emotional Processing:**
   - Development of new, non-threatening associations with trigger
   - Change in semantic meaning of feared stimulus
   - Integration of new corrective information
   - *Example:* "I can speak in front of people without catastrophe occurring"

3. **Extinction Learning:**
   - Brain learns that feared outcome does NOT occur
   - Creation of safety signal/memory
   - Inhibitory learning (not erasing original fear memory, but building new inhibitory association)

4. **Self-Efficacy Enhancement:**
   - Increased confidence in ability to cope with challenging situations
   - "I survived this, I can handle it"
   - Shift from "I can't" to "I can"
   - Generalization to other feared situations

#### 2.2 Five Phases of Exposure Therapy

**Phase 1: Assessment**
- Understand precise nature of anxiety
- Identify specific triggers
- Assess associated symptoms and manifestations
- Evaluate impact on functioning
- Measure baseline anxiety levels
- Create comprehensive "fear map"

**Phase 2: Fear Hierarchy Construction**
- List all feared social situations
- Rate each situation 0-100 for anxiety provocation
- Organize from least to most anxiety-provoking
- Identify both situational and performance fears
- Define specific behavioral goals

**Phase 3: Preparation & Psychoeducation**
- Explain anxiety mechanism and cycle
- Explain exposure therapy rationale
- Teach relaxation techniques (optional but helpful)
- Set realistic expectations
- Establish sense of safety and control

**Phase 4: Graduated Exposure**
- Begin with least anxiety-provoking items
- Maintain exposure until habituation occurs (20-30+ minutes)
- Progress to next item only after habituation achieved
- Controlled, predictable exposure
- Patient maintains control (can pause, slow down)

**Phase 5: Consolidation & Real-World Practice**
- Practice exposures outside therapy sessions
- Generalization to real-world situations
- Maintenance of gains
- Relapse prevention planning
- Building resilience and long-term coping

#### 2.3 Exposure Therapy Principles for SAD

**Gradual Progression:**
- Begin with situations causing moderate anxiety (not minimal, not maximal)
- Create explicit or dynamic hierarchy
- Build confidence through early successes
- Prevent overwhelming and dropout

**Repeated Exposure:**
- Single exposure rarely sufficient
- Multiple exposures needed for consolidation
- Practice both in-session and outside
- Typically 8-16 sessions recommended for SAD

**Sustained Exposure:**
- Sufficient duration (20-30+ minutes)
- Stop exposure when habituation occurs
- Not brief "dip in" approach
- Allows emotional processing to occur

**Response Prevention:**
- Refrain from safety behaviors during exposure
- Avoid escape/avoidance during the exposure
- Maintain exposure to the anxiety level
- Prevents reinforcement of avoidance patterns

**Predictability & Control:**
- Clear progression roadmap
- Patient knows what to expect
- Ability to pause/stop if needed (safety signal)
- Therapist support and presence

#### 2.4 Exposure Hierarchy Examples for SAD

**Mild Anxiety (20-40 SUDS):**
- Making brief eye contact with strangers
- Speaking one sentence in small group
- Ordering food with simple request
- Introducing yourself to one person
- Attending social event with supportive friend

**Moderate Anxiety (40-60 SUDS):**
- Speaking for 2-3 minutes in group setting
- Eating/drinking in presence of others
- Making a phone call to business
- Attending party where few people known
- Initiating conversation with acquaintance

**High Anxiety (60-80 SUDS):**
- Giving short presentation to group
- Speaking up frequently in meeting
- Eating meal alone in restaurant
- Attending party with mostly strangers
- Asking for something from authority figure

**Extreme Anxiety (80-100 SUDS):**
- Giving formal presentation to large audience
- Public speaking with evaluation/judgment
- Formal job interview
- High-stakes performance situation
- Speaking to multiple unfamiliar people

### 3. Virtual Reality in Exposure Therapy

#### 3.1 Why Virtual Reality is Effective for Exposure Therapy

**Advantages Over In Vivo Exposure:**

1. **Safety and Control:**
   - Completely safe environment (no actual social danger)
   - Therapist controls all variables
   - Predictable scenarios
   - Can reset/repeat scenarios immediately
   - Eliminates uncontrollable real-world factors (weather, others' behavior)

2. **Accessibility:**
   - No need for real audience, public venue, or confederates
   - Reduced logistical barriers
   - Can be delivered in clinic/home setting
   - Lower cost than arranging real-world exposures
   - Addresses accessibility for housebound patients

3. **Immersion and Presence:**
   - "Presence" = subjective sense of "being there"
   - Brain responds as if in actual situation (amygdala activates)
   - Physiological responses mirror real situations
   - Emotional engagement sufficient for fear extinction
   - Multiple sensory channels (visual, auditory, some haptic)

4. **Customization and Personalization:**
   - Scenarios tailored to individual fears
   - Adjustable difficulty parameters in real-time
   - Can modify audience reactions, difficulty level, number of people
   - Matches cognitive/behavioral fears specifically
   - Adaptive difficulty based on patient progress

5. **Measurement and Monitoring:**
   - Real-time biofeedback (heart rate, galvanic skin response)
   - Anxiety ratings throughout exposure
   - Performance metrics (duration, difficulty level, progress)
   - Objective data for therapist adjustment
   - Tracking of habituation curves

6. **Reduced Dropout Rates:**
   - Comparable or lower attrition than in vivo exposure
   - Emotional intensity more manageable
   - Perceived control reduces avoidance of therapy itself
   - Safety and confidentiality maintained

#### 3.2 Effectiveness Data for VR Exposure Therapy

**Meta-Analysis Findings:**

- **Effect size (Hedges' g):**
  - Post-intervention: -0.86 (-1.04 to -0.68) [Large effect]
  - 3 months: -1.03 (-1.35 to -0.72) [Very large effect]
  - 6 months: -1.14 (-1.39 to -0.89) [Very large effect]
  - 12 months: -0.74 (-1.05 to -0.43) [Large effect]

- **Attrition rates:** 11.36% average (low dropout despite challenges)

- **Symptom reduction:** 60-90% of patients achieve significant improvement

- **Mechanism confirmation:** In-session anxiety reduction predicts post-treatment improvement

**Comparison to In Vivo:**
- **Post-intervention:** Equivalent efficacy to in vivo exposure
- **3-12 month follow-up:** In vivo shows slightly better long-term maintenance
  - Likely due to transfer of learning to real environments
  - Real-world practice more directly applicable
  - Suggests need for bridge to real-world exposure after VR

**Adolescent Data:**
- Effective for school-based anxiety
- Significant reduction in state anxiety (η² = 0.74)
- Social anxiety symptom reduction (d = 0.82)
- Presence during VR associated with better outcomes
- Feasible and acceptable for younger populations

#### 3.3 Mechanisms of VR Effectiveness

**Presence and Immersion:**
- Sense of "being there" crucial for effectiveness
- Greater immersion = greater presence
- Greater presence = better fear activation = better extinction
- Multiple modalities enhance presence (visual, audio, interactive)

**Fear Activation:**
- VR must activate the fear network sufficiently
- Virtual environment perceived as realistic
- Audience/social stimuli must be believable
- Anxiety ratings should elevate in-session
- Without sufficient fear activation, habituation cannot occur

**Corrective Learning:**
- New safety information incorporated
- Expectancy violation (feared outcome doesn't occur)
- Emotional memory updated
- Automatic threat detection reduced

**Sense of Control:**
- Ability to pause/slow/stop exposure
- Therapist adjustments in real-time
- Predictable progression
- Reduces avoidance of therapy itself
- Enhances engagement and motivation

#### 3.4 Design Principles for Effective VR Exposure Therapy

**Technical/Visual Design:**

1. **Realism and Believability:**
   - Photorealistic graphics or good quality stylized graphics
   - Realistic lighting and shadows
   - Authentic materials and textures
   - Consistent physics and interactions
   - Attention to detail increases immersion

2. **Audio Design:**
   - Realistic ambient sounds
   - Appropriate background noise levels
   - Authentic dialogue/speech patterns
   - Responsive audio to player actions
   - Directional audio cues

3. **Interactive Elements:**
   - Responsive environment to user actions
   - People/avatars react realistically
   - Environmental changes based on user behavior
   - Feedback mechanisms
   - Natural interaction metaphors

**Clinical Design:**

1. **Scenario Authenticity:**
   - Based on actual feared situations
   - Include contextual details
   - Realistic spatial layouts
   - Believable social interactions
   - Multiple variations of similar scenario

2. **Progressive Difficulty:**
   - Clear hierarchy visible to patient
   - Manipulable parameters (audience size, difficulty, time)
   - Achievable early steps building confidence
   - Challenging but not overwhelming later steps

3. **Patient Safety and Control:**
   - Clear pause/exit mechanisms
   - Ability to adjust difficulty in real-time
   - Visual confidence meter
   - Supportive feedback
   - Safe base area (e.g., therapist office)

4. **Therapist Control:**
   - Real-time adjustment of exposure elements
   - Observation of patient behavior
   - Scenario selection and modification
   - Feedback delivery
   - Session pacing control

5. **Psychological Safety:**
   - Non-judgmental environment
   - Calm introduction space
   - Gradual escalation
   - Reassurance mechanisms
   - Support availability

#### 3.5 Challenges and Limitations of VR for Exposure Therapy

**Technical Challenges:**
- Simulator sickness (20-30% of users) - cybersickness from VR use
- Motion tracking errors causing disorientation
- Graphical limitations making scenarios feel unrealistic
- Hardware discomfort with prolonged use
- Technical failures disrupting flow

**Clinical Challenges:**
- Difficulty creating truly realistic social interactions
- Uncanny valley effect (people look "almost human" but not quite)
- Limited variation in avatar expressions/responses
- Harder to recreate nuanced social dynamics
- Some patients struggle to immerse despite technical quality

**User Experience Issues:**
- Vision complications (focusing problems, motion sickness)
- Difficulty communicating with therapist in headset
- Isolation from therapist during exposure
- Posture and physical strain
- Epilepsy risk from flickering/patterns

**Long-term Efficacy:**
- Slightly less effective at long-term follow-up than in vivo
- Transfer to real-world not automatic
- Bridge to real-world exposure often needed
- Some anxiety may return without real-world practice
- Requires combination with real-world behavioral experiments

**Solutions and Mitigation:**
- Gradual progression reduces simulator sickness
- Clear graphics and high frame rates minimize disorientation
- Multimodal design (audio, haptics) enhances presence
- Therapist presence/communication during exposure
- Bridge to real-world exposure as final stage
- Flexible hardware (hand controllers, movement freedom)

## PART 2: PROJECT REQUIREMENTS

### 4. VR Social Anxiety Exposure Therapy App - Complete Requirements Specification

#### 4.1 Project Overview

**Objective:** 
Develop a comprehensive, level-based VR application enabling guided exposure therapy for social anxiety disorder through progressive, customizable social scenarios with real-time monitoring and therapeutic support features.

**Target Users:**
- Primary: Adults (18+) with diagnosed or suspected social anxiety disorder
- Secondary: Mental health professionals (therapists, psychologists) conducting therapy
- Tertiary: Adolescents (16-17) with parental/therapist consent

**Use Context:**
- Clinical settings (therapist-guided)
- Home-based use (self-guided or therapist-monitored)
- Both synchronous (live therapist) and asynchronous (self-guided) modes

**Success Metrics:**
- Anxiety reduction: ≥50% reduction in mean SUDS across progression
- Engagement: ≥80% completion rate for Level 1-3
- Safety: <5% severe adverse events (simulator sickness)
- User satisfaction: ≥4/5 on usability and acceptability scales
- Clinical efficacy: Measurable improvement on standardized anxiety measures

### 5. Level-by-Level Requirements

#### 5.1 LEVEL 1: Foundation - One-on-One Conversation

**Clinical Rationale:**
- Begin with lowest anxiety-provoking scenario
- Single person interaction simplest social situation
- Build fundamental skills and habituation
- Establish sense of control and safety
- Baseline for progress measurement

**Scenario Description:**
- Virtual therapist office or calm waiting room
- Single NPC (non-player character) engaging in conversation
- Minimal social threat/judgment
- Focus on basic interaction and eye contact

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Familiarize with VR environment and controls
2. Practice sustained eye contact
3. Tolerate prolonged social interaction
4. Experience anxiety reduction through habituation
5. Build confidence for progression

**Anxiety Hierarchy (SUDS 15-40):**

| Sub-Level | Description | SUDS | Key Fears |
|-----------|-------------|------|-----------|
| 1A | Brief greeting with NPC (1 min) | 15-20 | Eye contact, speaking |
| 1B | Extended conversation with NPC (3-5 min) | 20-25 | Maintaining interaction |
| 1C | Conversation with topic change (5 min) | 25-30 | Responding naturally |
| 1D | Conversation with evaluative NPC (5 min) | 30-35 | Perceived judgment |
| 1E | Extended conversation, varied topics (8 min) | 35-40 | Sustained attention |

**Gameplay Elements:**
- NPC gives verbal feedback and asks follow-up questions
- Environmental navigation (simple movement)
- Topic selection via voice or interface menu
- Visual indicators of NPC's reaction (positive/neutral)
- Anxiety meter showing current SUDS level
- Progress tracker showing completion of sub-levels

**Technical Requirements - Level 1:**
- Single NPC model with facial animations
- 3-4 conversation trees with 15-20 dialogue branches
- Simple room environment (office, café, waiting room)
- Eye contact detection (optional but valuable)
- Real-time anxiety measurement interface
- Audio dialogue system with realistic voices
- Pause/safety mechanism clearly accessible

**Metrics & Progress Tracking:**
- Total duration of interaction attempted
- SUDS ratings at start, during (every 2 min), and end
- Eye contact duration and consistency
- Dialogue engagement (questions asked, responses given)
- Session completion status
- User comfort level self-report

**Progression Criteria:**
- Complete 1E with final SUDS <30 (habituation achieved)
- Maintain eye contact >50% of time
- Actively engage in conversation (ask questions, respond fully)
- Express readiness to proceed to Level 2
- OR 3+ sessions at this level with improving trend

**Difficulty Parameters (Therapist-Adjustable in Real-Time):**
- NPC eye contact level (staring vs. natural)
- NPC facial expressions (friendly vs. neutral vs. judgmental)
- Conversation topics (safe vs. personal vs. evaluative)
- Response time allowed (rushed vs. relaxed)
- Dialogue difficulty (simple vs. complex topics)

#### 5.2 LEVEL 2: Social Interaction - Small Group Conversation

**Clinical Rationale:**
- Step up complexity to multiple people
- Introduce performance anxiety element
- Practice with audience present but not focused
- Manage hypervigilance to multiple social cues
- Build social assertiveness

**Scenario Description:**
- Café, party, or workplace break room setting
- 3-5 NPCs engaged in conversation
- Patient joins existing conversation
- NPCs vary in attentiveness (some ignore, some monitor)
- Background activity and ambient conversation

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Manage anxiety with multiple social observers
2. Join existing conversation naturally
3. Contribute meaningfully to group discussion
4. Tolerate being "the center of attention"
5. Develop conversational skills in group setting

**Anxiety Hierarchy (SUDS 30-55):**

| Sub-Level | Description | SUDS | Key Fears |
|-----------|-------------|------|-----------|
| 2A | Join 2-person conversation (listening only) | 30-35 | Being observed |
| 2B | Ask question to join 3-person group | 35-40 | Interrupting, speaking |
| 2C | Share opinion in 3-4 person group | 40-45 | Evaluation, judgment |
| 2D | Lead brief topic in 4-5 person group | 45-50 | Being center of attention |
| 2E | Active participation in group discussion | 50-55 | Sustained social scrutiny |

**Gameplay Elements:**
- Multiple NPC models with independent behaviors
- Branching dialogue system with group dynamics
- Conversation topics appear as menu options or voice commands
- Visual indicators of group's attention level
- NPC acknowledgment/validation when patient speaks
- Ambient sounds and background activity
- "Presence level" meter showing group attention
- Anxiety escalation visible (NPCs react to nervousness)

**Technical Requirements - Level 2:**
- 5-8 distinct NPC models
- Complex conversation system with NPC-to-NPC dialogue
- Group dynamics system (attention following, acknowledgment)
- Environmental setting (café/party with props and ambient characters)
- Multi-directional audio (hearing NPCs from different positions)
- Gesture and body language system for NPCs
- Advanced eye contact tracking (multiple targets)
- Real-time attention feedback system

**Metrics & Progress Tracking:**
- Speaking frequency and duration
- Topics initiated vs. responded to
- Eye contact distribution among group
- SUDS progression during session
- Anxiety management effectiveness
- Conversational reciprocity measures
- Number of questions asked/answered

**Progression Criteria:**
- Complete 2E with final SUDS <45
- Initiate at least 3 new conversational topics
- Maintain appropriate eye contact with multiple people
- Participate actively (>40% talking time vs. 60% listening)
- Express confidence in small group settings
- OR 3-4 sessions showing improving habituation

**Difficulty Parameters (Therapist-Adjustable):**
- Group size (2 to 5 people)
- Group composition (friendly vs. neutral vs. critical)
- Attention focus (ignoring patient vs. monitoring vs. scrutinizing)
- Topic difficulty (light conversation vs. evaluative)
- Group response to patient input (validating vs. ignoring vs. critical)
- Ambient noise level
- Movement freedom in environment

#### 5.3 LEVEL 3: Performance Anxiety - Public Speaking

**Clinical Rationale:**
- Address most common SAD scenario
- Sustained performance under scrutiny
- High physiological arousal component
- Testing anxiety and behavioral exposure together
- Real-world applicability (presentations, meetings)

**Scenario Description:**
- Classroom, conference room, or auditorium setting
- Audience of varying size (scalable)
- Patient gives presentation on selected topic
- Audience exhibits realistic behaviors (attention, boredom, skepticism)
- Real-time feedback and evaluation component

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Tolerate public speaking anxiety
2. Present information clearly despite nervousness
3. Manage audience attention and potential judgment
4. Develop public speaking skills
5. Overcome catastrophic expectations about performance

**Anxiety Hierarchy (SUDS 40-75):**

| Sub-Level | Description | SUDS | Key Fears |
|-----------|-------------|------|-----------|
| 3A | Present to 5 audience members (3-5 min) | 40-45 | Being watched |
| 3B | Present to 15 people (5 min) | 45-55 | Visible anxiety |
| 3C | Present to 30 people with varied reactions | 55-65 | Negative evaluation |
| 3D | Present with challenging questions | 65-70 | Not knowing answers |
| 3E | Present to 50+ people with high scrutiny | 70-75 | Complete failure fear |

**Gameplay Elements:**
- Full presentation interface (slides, notes, timer)
- Variable audience reactions (nodding, yawning, skeptical looks)
- Audience size clearly visible
- Real-time performance metrics (pacing, clarity, filler words)
- Audience feedback/questions during/after presentation
- Mistake tolerance (stumbles don't cause failure)
- Visual anxiety indicators (shaking hand display, voice quaver audio)
- Accomplishment celebration upon completion

**Technical Requirements - Level 3:**
- Large-scale audience model (scalable rendering)
- Audience animation system (attention following, expressions)
- Presentation interface with slides and notes
- Microphone input and voice detection system
- Speech analysis (pacing, filler words, clarity) - optional
- Audience reaction system (clapping, questioning, skeptical looks)
- Performance metrics display
- Multiple presentation topics (6-10 options)
- Lighting for stage/presentation area
- Timer and pacing feedback

**Metrics & Progress Tracking:**
- Presentation completion (full vs. partial)
- Speaking duration and pacing (words per minute)
- Anxiety trajectory (start SUDS, minimum, final)
- Stumbles/recovery capability
- Filler word frequency
- Audience attention maintenance
- Questions answered successfully
- Self-perceived performance vs. actual

**Progression Criteria:**
- Complete 3E, maintaining speech despite anxiety
- Finish presentations without escape/avoidance
- SUDS habituation during presentation (start 70, end <55)
- Handle audience skepticism/difficult questions
- Positive self-evaluation of performance
- Express readiness for real-world presentation
- OR 4-5 sessions with increasing comfort

**Difficulty Parameters (Therapist-Adjustable in Real-Time):**
- Audience size (5 to 500+)
- Audience composition (supportive, neutral, hostile)
- Audience behaviors (attentive, inattentive, disruptive)
- Question difficulty (friendly, challenging, critical)
- Topic selection (safe vs. personal vs. evaluative)
- Time pressure (relaxed vs. strict timing)
- Visibility of patient anxiety (hidden vs. visible tremors/voice)
- Audience evaluation type (constructive vs. judgmental)

- SDS (Sheehan Disability Scale) - functional impairment
